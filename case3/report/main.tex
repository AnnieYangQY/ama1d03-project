\section*{(a) Descriptions of the Defined Contribution Plan and Its Application in the Pension System}

\subsection*{1. Defined Contribution (DC) versus Defined Benefit (DB)}

In a defined contribution plan, contributions are set in advance but the retirement benefit is not. The employer and employee pay into a personal account, and the final balance depends on how much was contributed, how investments performed, and what fees were charged. The member carries most of the investment and longevity risk.

In a defined benefit plan, the situation is reversed. The benefit formula is fixed, but the contributions needed to fund it are not. The sponsor uses actuarial assumptions to set contribution levels and bears most of the risk if the plan falls short. Hong Kong also has Occupational Retirement Schemes that can be run on either a DC or DB basis. These sit alongside mandatory MPF as part of the second pillar.

Hong Kong's Mandatory Provident Fund System began on \textbf{1 December 2000}. It is a compulsory DC scheme and forms the second pillar of the city's retirement framework:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{First pillar:} Flat-rate cash benefits subject to means tests. CSSA is the last-resort safety net. OALA targets lower-income seniors. OAA is more widely available but pays less.
  \item \textbf{Second pillar:} Mandatory and voluntary MPF contributions, together with ORSO where it applies.
  \item \textbf{Third pillar:} Personal savings, insurance, and family support.
\end{itemize}

Hong Kong has no public pension linked to past earnings. First-pillar payments are fixed amounts, so MPF is the main retirement base for the middle class. This is a major difference from systems such as Canada's CPP or US Social Security.

Each employee holds a personal account within a scheme. When changing jobs, accrued benefits can be transferred to a new employer's scheme or kept in a personal account.

\subsection*{3. Mandatory and Voluntary Contributions}

\textbf{Mandatory Contributions:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Employee and employer each contribute \textbf{5\%} of relevant income.
  \item Relevant income has a monthly floor of HK\$7,100 and a ceiling of HK\$30,000.
  \item If monthly income falls below HK\$7,100, the employee pays nothing, but the employer still contributes 5\% of HK\$7,100.
  \item The maximum mandatory contribution from either party is HK\$1,500 per month.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Voluntary Contributions:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{In-scheme VC} is paid into the existing MPF plan. It does not qualify for salaries-tax relief and follows the same withdrawal rules as mandatory contributions.
  \item \textbf{Tax Deductible Voluntary Contributions (TVC)} are held in a separate personal account. Up to \textbf{HK\$60,000 per year} can be deducted for salaries tax, and the withdrawal rules are more flexible.
\end{itemize}

In Case III, Peter and Mary each contribute HK\$1,000, which is 5\% of a HK\$20,000 salary. Their employers match this amount, so total monthly contributions are \textbf{HK\$2,000}.

\subsection*{4. Benefits, Withdrawal, and Retirement Age}

Members can normally withdraw accrued benefits at age \textbf{65}. Benefits may also be taken early on permanent departure from Hong Kong, total incapacity, terminal illness, or retirement at age 60 after leaving employment. Members with total accrued benefits of HK\$5,000 or less may apply for small-balance withdrawal. On death, benefits are paid to nominated beneficiaries.

At retirement, members may take a lump sum or convert part of the balance into an annuity through the MPF platform. Annuities help address longevity risk, which is one of the main weaknesses of DC plans when members live longer than their savings last.

\subsection*{5. Investment Options, Returns, and Fees}

MPF schemes offer Hong Kong, China, Asia, and global equity funds, mixed-asset funds, bond and guaranteed funds, and index funds such as the Tracker Fund of Hong Kong.

Members bear investment risk. Fund managers charge annual fees, usually between 0.5\% and 2\%, which reduce net returns over long periods. In Case III, Peter invests in a global fund and Mary in a Hong Kong fund. This shows how fund choice affects outcomes even when the contribution pattern is the same.

\subsection*{6. Tax Treatment and System Role}

Mandatory MPF contributions are tax-exempt up to statutory limits. TVC gives members an extra tax incentive if they want to close a retirement income gap. Research and industry practice both suggest that mandatory contributions alone are \textbf{not enough} to maintain pre-retirement living standards. Members are expected to add voluntary savings and, where they qualify, government cash benefits.

\section*{(b) Compare and Analyze the Defined Contribution Plan: Peter and Mary Examples}

\subsection*{1. Modelling Setup}

We simulate monthly MPF accumulation from December 2000 until each member reaches age 65. Table~\ref{tab:assumptions} lists the main assumptions. Monthly fund returns and CPI are taken from the sources described in Table~\ref{tab:sources}.

\textbf{Notes on the assumptions:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Salary growth linked to CPI.} This treats real wages as flat over a member's career. In practice, wages often rise faster than inflation early in a career, which would increase final salary and push the replacement ratio down further.
  \item \textbf{Investment returns.} We use actual monthly returns up to the latest available data, then assume \textbf{4\% per year} on a nominal basis thereafter. This matches common long-run planning figures used by the MPFA and industry. The model is not a pure historical back-test. Global funds outperformed Hong Kong equities over 2000--2025, but that reflects one historical window and should not be read as a forecast.
  \item \textbf{4\% withdrawal rule.} Retirement income in the first year equals 4\% of the account balance, with later withdrawals assumed to rise with inflation. The rule was developed for 30-year retirements in the United States. Hong Kong residents often live beyond 85, so a fixed withdrawal rate can leave members exposed to running out of money.
  \item \textbf{Fees.} We assume 1\% per year, which sits in the middle of the MPF range. Index funds often charge less than 0.5\%, while active equity funds commonly charge between 1.2\% and 1.8\%. Over 25 to 45 years, even small fee differences matter.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{3. Advantages and Disadvantages}

\textbf{Advantages:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Individual ownership and portability.} Accounts belong to the member and move with them, which suits Hong Kong's mobile labour market. There is no employer credit risk of the kind seen in DB plans.
  \item \textbf{Employer matching.} The employer's 5\% contribution doubles savings without extra cost to the employee.
  \item \textbf{Investment choice.} Members can choose funds that match their risk tolerance.
  \item \textbf{Tax relief on TVC.} Up to HK\$60,000 of TVC per year is tax-deductible.
  \item \textbf{Long compounding horizons.} Members who start early benefit most. At 4\% per year, the Rule of 72 suggests wealth doubles about every 18 years. Over 45 years that compounds to roughly six times the original sum, compared with about 2.7 times over 25 years.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Disadvantages:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Market risk and sequence risk.} Members bear the full downside. A sharp fall in markets just before retirement can cut the final balance sharply.
  \item \textbf{No pooling of risk.} Unlike DB plans, DC plans do not share longevity or investment shortfalls across members.
  \item \textbf{Low replacement ratios.} Even Peter reaches only 36\% on mandatory contributions, below the 60--70\% range often used in financial planning.
  \item \textbf{Fees and poor decisions.} Management fees compound against the member over time, and frequent fund switching often reduces returns further.
  \item \textbf{Penalty for late starters.} Mary's 9.1\% replacement ratio shows that beginning at age 40 cannot be fixed simply by picking a better fund.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{(c) Supporting Retirement Under DC with the Social Security System}

\subsection*{1. Hong Kong Three-Pillar Framework}

Unlike Canada's CPP or US Social Security, Hong Kong has no universal pension tied to earnings. Government support is means-tested and works in layers:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The \textbf{first pillar} gives a basic floor to seniors with low assets and low income. CSSA has the strictest tests, OALA is subject to asset and income limits, and OAA is easier to qualify for but pays less.
  \item The \textbf{second pillar} is mandatory MPF for employed workers.
  \item The \textbf{third pillar} is where most people must look if they want to keep their standard of living in retirement.
\end{itemize}

For middle-income workers like Peter and Mary, first-pillar benefits are largely ruled out once MPF balances count toward the asset test. Retirement income has to come from MPF and voluntary savings, not from stacking pillars on top of one another.

\subsection*{2. Old Age Living Allowance}

Hong Kong residents aged 65 or above may receive Normal OALA of HK\$2,065 per month or Higher OALA of HK\$4,185 per month in 2025/26, provided they pass income and asset tests. For a single person, the asset limit for both tiers is about HK\$401,000. \textbf{MPF benefits that have not yet been withdrawn count as assets.} The more a member saves in a DC plan, the less likely they are to receive OALA. The two sources of support do not simply add together.

\subsection*{4. Illustrative Strategies}

\textbf{Peter, retiring in 2045:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item MPF already gives a reasonable base at 36\%, but he will not qualify for OALA given his expected MPF assets.
  \item Adding about HK\$1,600 per month through \textbf{TVC} (tax-deductible voluntary contributions, as defined in Section~(a)) would bring MPF income up to a 60\% replacement ratio within the annual tax-deductible limit of HK\$60,000.
  \item A lifecycle approach makes more sense than holding global equity for all 45 years. Higher equity exposure early on, with a gradual shift toward bonds and capital-preservation funds after age 55, would reduce sequence risk.
  \item Converting part of the MPF balance into an annuity would provide a guaranteed income floor and help manage longevity risk.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Mary, retiring in 2025:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Mandatory MPF alone leaves her at 9.1\%. Her balance of HK\$851,000 is above the OALA asset limit of HK\$401,000, so she will not receive OALA either.
  \item Working until age 70 raises her replacement ratio only to 10.9\% in our simulation. Extra years of contributions help, but they cannot make up for starting 20 years later than Peter.
  \item The savings she needs are far above the TVC tax cap. She would need non-deductible VC, third-pillar savings, and possibly part-time work.
  \item With a smaller account, annuitising part of the balance or delaying withdrawals could still improve income security.
\end{itemize}

A workable plan for Mary might combine MPF income of HK\$2,836 with third-pillar savings or part-time work of about HK\$5,000 per month, giving roughly HK\$7,836 per month. That remains below the 60\% target of HK\$18,706. Even in the hypothetical Higher OALA case in Table~\ref{tab:income_hyp}, she would reach only 22.5\%.

Hong Kong's DC system places most retirement responsibility on the individual. Peter's case shows that starting early and contributing steadily is the strongest lever available. \subsection*{6. Core Conclusions}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Mandatory MPF alone is not enough to protect pre-retirement living standards. Voluntary savings are necessary.
  \item \textbf{When contributions begin} matters more than fund choice in a DC plan, because time drives compounding more than return differences do.
  \item Hong Kong's first pillar is a safety net for low-asset retirees, not a top-up for middle-income members with substantial MPF balances.
  \item A sound retirement plan combines mandatory MPF, TVC within tax limits, third-pillar savings, gradual de-risking near retirement, and partial annuitisation.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Key Formulae}

Future value with CPI-linked contributions, monthly simulation:
\begin{equation}
  FV = \sum_{t=1}^{n} PMT_0 (1+g)^{t-1} \prod_{k=t+1}^{n}(1+r_k)
\end{equation}
where $PMT_0$ is the initial monthly contribution, $g$ is the annual growth rate of contributions, $r_k$ is the net return in period $k$, and $n$ is the number of contribution periods.

When $r$ and $g$ are constant and $r \neq g$, the closed form for a growing annuity is:
\begin{equation}
  FV = PMT_0 (1+r) \cdot \frac{(1+r)^n - (1+g)^n}{r - g}
\end{equation}

Replacement ratio under a 4\% withdrawal rate:
\begin{equation}
  RR = \frac{FV \times 0.04}{Salary_{final}}
\end{equation}

To find the monthly TVC needed for a target replacement ratio $\rho^*$, solve for $PMT_{TVC}$ in
\begin{equation}
  \rho^* = \frac{0.04 \times FV(PMT_0, g, r, n) + 0.04 \times FV(PMT_{TVC}, g, r, n')}{Salary_{final}}
\end{equation}
where $n'$ is the number of periods over which extra contributions are paid. In this case, $n'$ equals the full contribution horizon. Table~\ref{tab:tvc} is produced by binary search in the simulation model.

Monthly income gap to a 60\% target:
\begin{equation}
  Gap_{monthly} = 0.60 \times \frac{Salary_{final}}{12} - \frac{FV_{MPF} \times 0.04}{12}
\end{equation}
