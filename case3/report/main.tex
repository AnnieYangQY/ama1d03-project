\section*{(a) DC Plan and MPF in Hong Kong's Pension System}

\subsection*{1. DC versus DB}

In a \textbf{defined contribution (DC)} plan, contributions are fixed but the retirement benefit is not. The final balance depends on contributions, investment returns, and fees, and the member bears most investment and longevity risk. In a \textbf{defined benefit (DB)} plan, the benefit formula is fixed while required contributions are not; the sponsor bears most funding risk. Hong Kong's MPF, launched \textbf{1 December 2000}, is a mandatory DC scheme and forms the \textbf{second pillar}, together with ORSO where applicable.

Hong Kong has no public pension linked to past earnings. The \textbf{first pillar} provides flat-rate, means-tested cash benefits: CSSA as the last-resort safety net, OALA for lower-income seniors, and OAA which is more widely available but pays less. The \textbf{third pillar} is personal savings, insurance, and family support. For the middle class, MPF is the main retirement base.

\subsection*{2. MPF Rules Relevant to Case III}

The MPFA classifies schemes into master trust, employer-sponsored, and industry schemes. Each member holds a portable personal account.

\textbf{Mandatory contributions:} employee and employer each pay \textbf{5\%} of relevant income, with a monthly floor of HK\$7,100 and ceiling of HK\$30,000. If income is below HK\$7,100, the employee pays nothing but the employer still contributes 5\% of HK\$7,100. The maximum from either party is HK\$1,500 per month.

\textbf{Voluntary contributions:} \textbf{In-scheme VC} has no salaries-tax relief and follows mandatory withdrawal rules. \textbf{Tax-deductible voluntary contributions (TVC)} are held in a separate account; up to \textbf{HK\$60,000 per year} is deductible for salaries tax.

Members can normally withdraw at age \textbf{65}, or earlier on permanent departure, incapacity, or terminal illness. They may take a lump sum or convert part of the balance into an annuity to manage longevity risk. Fund fees are typically 0.5\%--2\% per year. In Case III, Peter and Mary each contribute HK\$1,000 with employer matching, totalling \textbf{HK\$2,000} per month. Peter invests in a global equity fund and Mary in a Hong Kong equity fund. Mandatory contributions alone are \textbf{not enough} to maintain pre-retirement living standards.

\subsection*{3. Advantages and Disadvantages}

\textbf{Advantages:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Individual ownership and portability suit Hong Kong's mobile labour market.
  \item Employer matching doubles savings without extra employee cost.
  \item Members can choose funds aligned with risk tolerance.
  \item TVC of up to HK\$60,000 per year is tax-deductible.
  \item Early starters benefit most from compounding; at 4\% p.a., wealth roughly doubles every 18 years.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Disadvantages:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Members bear full market and sequence risk.
  \item Unlike DB plans, there is no pooling of longevity or investment shortfalls.
  \item Even Peter reaches only 36\% replacement on mandatory contributions.
  \item Fees compound against members over 25--45 years.
  \item Mary's 9.1\% ratio shows that starting at 40 cannot be fixed by fund choice alone.
\end{enumerate}

Near retirement, members may adjust fund allocation: Peter can shift from global equity toward bonds and capital-preservation funds after age 55; Mary may move from Hong Kong equity toward mixed-asset funds to reduce volatility. Part~(c) builds on these balances and examines how MPF can be combined with OALA.

\section*{(c) Social Security and Recommendations}

\subsection*{1. Three Pillars and OALA}

Part~(c) asks how DC retirees combine MPF with first-pillar OALA. OALA pays Normal allowance of HK\$2,065 or Higher allowance of HK\$4,185 per month in 2025/26, subject to income and asset tests. For a single person, assessable assets are capped at about HK\$401,000 and monthly assessable income at about HK\$10,700 for Higher OALA.

\textbf{Unwithdrawn MPF counts as assets.} To use OALA alongside MPF, retirees may need to \textbf{structure benefits at age 65}. We assume \textbf{Mary annuitises her MPF} through the MPF platform so benefits are taken as a pension stream rather than a retained lump sum, keeping assessable liquid assets below the cap. Her MPF pension of HK\$2,836 per month is below the income limit, so she qualifies for \textbf{Higher OALA of HK\$4,185}. \textbf{Peter} has a balance of HK\$4.99 million and MPF drawdown of HK\$16,655 per month, so he fails both the asset and income tests and receives no OALA.

\subsection*{3. Strategies and Conclusions}

\textbf{Peter, retiring in 2045:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item MPF provides a 36\% base but OALA is unavailable.
  \item Add about HK\$1,600/month through TVC to reach 60\% within the tax-deductible limit.
  \item Shift gradually toward bonds and capital-preservation funds after age 55 to reduce sequence risk.
  \item Consider partial annuitisation for longevity protection.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Mary, retiring in 2025:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Annuitise MPF at 65} and apply for \textbf{Higher OALA} to raise combined income to HK\$7,021 per month, 22.5\% replacement.
  \item OALA helps but the gap to 60\% remains large; add TVC where affordable and third-pillar savings or part-time work.
  \item Delaying retirement to 70 raises MPF-only RR only to 10.9\% (Table~\ref{tab:delay}).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Core conclusions:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Mandatory MPF alone cannot maintain pre-retirement living standards.
  \item \textbf{When contributions begin} matters more than fund choice.
  \item OALA can supplement MPF for members like Mary who structure benefits to meet the tests; high-balance members like Peter rely on MPF and voluntary savings instead.
  \item A complete plan combines MPF, TVC within tax limits, third-pillar savings, lifecycle de-risking, and partial annuitisation.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Key Formulae}

Future value with CPI-linked contributions:
\begin{equation}
  FV = \sum_{t=1}^{n} PMT_0 (1+g)^{t-1} \prod_{k=t+1}^{n}(1+r_k)
\end{equation}
When $r$ and $g$ are constant and $r \neq g$,
\begin{equation}
  FV = PMT_0 (1+r) \cdot \frac{(1+r)^n - (1+g)^n}{r - g}
\end{equation}
Replacement ratio and monthly gap to a 60\% target:
\begin{equation}
  RR = \frac{FV \times 0.04}{Salary_{final}}, \qquad
  Gap_{monthly} = 0.60 \times \frac{Salary_{final}}{12} - \frac{FV \times 0.04}{12}
\end{equation}
where $PMT_0$ is initial monthly contribution, $g$ is contribution growth, $r_k$ is net return in period $k$, and $n$ is the number of periods.
